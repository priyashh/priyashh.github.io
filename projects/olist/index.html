The Objective

Piecing together a fragmented e-commerce ecosystem

Olist provided a massive incredibly messy database scattered across eight different tables. My objective wasn't just to clean the raw numbers but to extract the underlying business reality. I needed to map out the entire customer journey from the moment a credit card is swiped to the final delivery review to understand what makes the platform tick and where it is bleeding money.

By joining these datasets in SQL and modeling them visually I created an interactive narrative focused on four key pillars: revenue generation logistics geographic risks and supplier health.

Dashboard 01

Where the money flows and how people pay

Mapping out the sales data reveals a very clear hierarchy. Health and Beauty dominates the marketplace as the undeniable top earner with home goods and watches trailing behind. But the way Brazilians pay for these items is just as fascinating because credit cards power almost 80 percent of the entire economy.

When we look closely at installment plans a distinct behavioral trend emerges. Shoppers aren't using ten month payment plans for everyday items. They are specifically leveraging them to finance high ticket premium purchases. Meanwhile the state of São Paulo acts as the platform's economic engine single-handedly generating more cash than the next four states combined.

Dashboard 02

The unforgiving reality of shipping delays

If there is one chart that defines the success or failure of a transaction it is the relationship between delivery times and customer satisfaction. The data shows absolute zero forgiveness for bad logistics. When packages arrive even a day early shoppers happily award four or five stars. But the exact moment an order misses its deadline the feedback immediately tanks.

While Olist maintains a respectable 93 percent on time delivery rate overall this sharp penalty for lateness highlights how fragile customer trust really is. One logistical hiccup is all it takes to ruin the relationship forever.

Dashboard 03

A massive platform-wide retention crisis

Diving into the user demographics exposes a glaring vulnerability in the business model. An alarming 96.8 percent of people buy something once and then completely disappear. There is virtually no repeat buyer loyalty meaning Olist's growth relies entirely on the incredibly expensive process of constantly acquiring brand new shoppers.

Furthermore the platform's geographic footprint is incredibly unbalanced. São Paulo alone houses over 40 thousand unique buyers. While that massive volume is great having your entire customer base concentrated in one single state makes the company dangerously exposed to regional economic shifts.

Dashboard 04

Identifying the merchants doing the heavy lifting

Looking closely at the supply side the platform is essentially carried by a tiny fraction of elite sellers. While thousands of merchants are registered the highest grossing individual pulled in nearly 46 thousand Reais by strategically selling premium items rather than pushing cheap volume. Another merchant managed to ship over eighteen hundred individual orders entirely on their own.

Interestingly the data proves that biggest doesn't always mean best. The merchants pushing the absolute highest volumes out of São Paulo actually suffer from significantly worse customer satisfaction ratings compared to smaller more focused sellers in neighboring states.

Core Insights

The undeniable truths hidden in the dataset

Logistics dictate reputation

The correlation is absolute. Early arrivals guarantee praise but missing a delivery date by even 24 hours permanently destroys the seller's rating.

An endless acquisition treadmill

With a 97 percent churn rate there is no built in loyalty. The platform survives purely by replacing lost buyers with new ones.

The São Paulo dependency

Whether you measure by active users merchants or gross revenue this one specific region completely controls the platform's fate.

Financing premium goods

Installment plans are a strategic tool for the buyer. Maxing out a ten month payment plan is directly tied to the most expensive items in the catalog.

Volume vs quality

Scaling up orders often degrades the buyer experience. The most prolific shippers frequently have lower satisfaction scores than smaller regional competitors.

The Technical Pipeline

How the analysis was executed

The raw information was split across eight isolated CSV files covering nearly 100 thousand transactions. I designed a relational schema and wrote custom SQL queries to unify the order history calculate precise delivery delays and aggregate geographic performance metrics.

After validating the mathematical logic and identifying the strongest narrative angles in Excel I imported the refined dataset into Power BI. From there I engineered the cross filtering mechanics and designed the final layout to present a clear executive level story.
