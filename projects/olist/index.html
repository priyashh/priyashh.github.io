The Goal

Move beyond a big table of transactions.

Olist's raw dataset is 8 relational tables — orders, items, payments, reviews, customers, sellers, products, and geolocation. The goal was to join and model this data into a structured analytical layer, then build a Power BI dashboard that answers the questions a business leader would actually ask: Where is revenue coming from? Why do customers leave bad reviews? Who are the top-performing sellers?

The output is a 4-page interactive dashboard — Revenue Deep Dive, Delivery & Operations, Customer & Geography, and Seller Intelligence — each telling a distinct part of the business story.

Dashboard 01 — Revenue Deep Dive

Health & Beauty leads. Credit cards dominate. São Paulo is the engine.

The treemap immediately reveals the revenue hierarchy: Health & Beauty is the single largest category, followed by Bed, Bath & Table and Watches & Gifts. These three categories alone represent the core of Olist's product economy. The remaining long-tail of categories is significant but fragmented.

On the payment side, 78.3% of all revenue flows through credit cards, with boleto (Brazil's bank slip payment) at 17.9%. The installments chart tells a behaviorally interesting story — average order value climbs sharply with installment count, peaking at 10-installment orders. This signals that high-ticket items are the primary driver of installment usage, not impulse buys.

Geographically, São Paulo state generates over R$1M in revenue — more than the next four states combined. Rio de Janeiro and Minas Gerais follow at a significant distance.

Revenue Deep Dive — category treemap, payment type split, state-level revenue, and installment behavior.

Dashboard 02 — Delivery & Operations

Deliver early, earn a 4+ score. Deliver late, watch it crater.

The scatter plot of review score vs. delivery delay days is the most analytically striking visual in the dashboard. The curve is brutal and asymmetric: orders delivered 10–30 days early average a score of ~4.1, on-time orders hover around 4.0, but orders just 0–10 days late drop to ~2.7, and orders delayed 30+ days bottom out near 1.5.

The delivery bucket bar chart reinforces this: early delivery consistently earns 4+ scores across both "Early" and "Very Early" buckets. The cliff happens the moment an order crosses zero — any lateness is penalized disproportionately by customers.

The platform's overall on-time rate is 93.4%, with an average delivery time of 12.5 days. At the state level, AM and RO lead with 97%+ on-time rates, while RS and MT trail. The year slider (2016–2018) allows trend analysis — useful for spotting whether operational performance improved over time.

Delivery & Operations — on-time rate gauge, avg delivery days, delay vs. review score scatter, and per-state performance breakdown.

Dashboard 03 — Customer & Geography

96.9% one-time buyers. São Paulo carries almost half the platform.

The most striking finding on this page is the one-time vs. repeat customer split: 96.88% of customers placed only one order. This is a critical retention signal — Olist's growth model is almost entirely acquisition-driven, not loyalty-driven. The cancellation map shows cancellation clusters concentrated in the southeast, mirroring where order density is highest.

São Paulo state alone accounts for 40,302 unique customers and ~42,000 orders — more than the next three states combined. Rio de Janeiro (12,384) and Minas Gerais (11,259) are the only other states with meaningful scale. The revenue map confirms the same southeast concentration pattern.

For a business, this geographic skew is both an asset and a risk — Olist's performance is heavily tied to SP's economic conditions and logistics infrastructure.

Customer & Geography — total customers, one-time vs. repeat split, state-level customer counts, cancellation map, and order distribution map.

Dashboard 04 — Seller Intelligence

São Paulo sellers generate R$1.75M. The top performer handles 1,800 orders solo.

The seller intelligence page surfaces the supply-side dynamics. SP-based sellers contribute R$1.75M in total revenue — more than all other states combined. PR and MG follow at R$0.25M and R$0.20M respectively, showing a sharp concentration at the top.

Across 3,000 active sellers, the average order count per seller is 32 — but the seller performance table tells a more nuanced story. The top seller processed 1,806 orders for R$40,094 in revenue, while the highest-grossing single seller earned R$45,894 from 1,132 orders — a higher revenue-per-order ratio that suggests premium product positioning.

The state-level review chart shows RS and MG sellers consistently outscoring SP sellers — interesting given SP's volume dominance. High volume doesn't automatically mean high satisfaction on the seller side, which creates a quality vs. scale tension worth monitoring.

Key Findings

What the data actually said.

Finding 01

Late delivery tanks reviews — fast

Even 0–10 days late drops average review scores from 4.0+ to ~2.7. The damage is immediate and steep. Early delivery, on the other hand, earns consistent 4+ scores.

Finding 02

97% of customers never come back

96.88% of all customers are one-time buyers. The platform's growth is almost entirely dependent on new customer acquisition, not retention or repeat purchase.

Finding 03

São Paulo is the platform

SP generates 42K+ orders, 40K+ customers, and R$1.75M in seller revenue — more than all other states combined. Geographic concentration is extreme.

Finding 04

High installments = high order value

Average order value peaks at 10-installment payments (~R$420). Customers using more installments are buying higher-ticket items, not just spreading smaller purchases.

Finding 05

Health & Beauty leads revenue

Health & Beauty is the single largest revenue category, with Bed, Bath & Table and Watches & Gifts rounding out the top 3. These categories anchor Olist's product economy.

Finding 06

Volume sellers ≠ best reviewed sellers

SP sellers dominate volume but score lower on reviews than RS and MG sellers. Scale and satisfaction don't automatically align — a quality vs. growth tension worth tracking.

Methodology

How it was built.

01

Data Acquisition & Schema Mapping

Ingested 8 relational CSV tables from the Olist public dataset (99K+ orders). Mapped foreign key relationships across orders, items, payments, customers, sellers, products, reviews, and geolocation tables before writing any queries.

02

SQL Data Modeling & Cleaning

Wrote SQL queries to join tables, calculate delivery delay days, assign delivery buckets (Early/On-Time/Late), aggregate revenue by category and state, and derive seller-level KPIs. Handled nulls in delivery dates and filtered incomplete records before analysis.

03

Exploratory Analysis in Excel

Used Excel for preliminary distributions — review score histograms, category revenue shares, and installment behavior patterns — to validate SQL output and identify the most analytically interesting angles before building the final dashboard.

04

Power BI Dashboard Design

Built a 4-page interactive Power BI report with cross-page filters, map visuals for geographic analysis, a scatter plot for delivery-vs-satisfaction correlation, treemaps for category revenue, and KPI cards for executive-level summary metrics.
